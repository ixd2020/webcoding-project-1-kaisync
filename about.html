ABOUT THE ARTIST




"I paint my own reality. The only thing I know is that I paint because I need to, and I paint whatever passes through my head without any other consideration."

FRIDA KAHLO

was an artist known for her portraits. Kahlo was her own most popular subject, and had other works inspired by Mexican nature and artifacts. Kahlo's art was often inspired by either the country's popular culture or her own life.

In 1925, Kahlo was traveling on a bus when it crashed into a tramcar, severely injuring her. Spending several months with limited mobility and learning to cope with the boredom of her recovery process, Kahlo soon began to paint with the water-colours of her father, the talented photographer Guillermo Kahlo. Thus her interest in becoming an artist grew.

Kahlo met fellow Mexican artist Diego Rivera through the Mexican Communist Party in 1927 due to her interest in politics and art. The two artists got married in 1929, and spent the late 1920s and early 1930s travelling in Mexico and the United States.

In this time, Kahlo developed her artistic style by drawing inspiration from Mexican folk culture. She painted small self-portraits with elements from pre-Columbian and Catholic beliefs. The work of Kahlo caught the attention of the Surrealist artist André Breton who arranged for her first solo exhibition in New York in 1938, followed by another exhibition in Paris in 1939. The latter was less of a success, however the Louvre purchased The Frame from Kahlo. She was the first Mexican artist to be featured in their collection.

In the 1940s, Kahlo participated in Mexican and American art exhibitions, and taught art at the Escuela Nacional de Pintura, Escultura y Grabado ("La Esmeralda") and was a founding member of the Seminario de Cultura Mexicana. Her first solo Mexican exhibition was held in 1953, a year before her passing.
